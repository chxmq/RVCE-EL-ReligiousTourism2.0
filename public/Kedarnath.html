About Kedarnath

Kedarnath Dham, nestled in the Rudraprayag district of Uttarakhand, stands as one of the most significant places of worship dedicated to Lord Shiva. Surrounded by majestic snow-clad peaks, lush meadows, and dense forests of the lower Himalayan ranges, Kedarnath is located near the source of the Mandakini River at an altitude of 3,584 meters. The temple, a part of the sacred Chota Char Dham Yatra, is one of the 12 Jyotirlingas and holds a prominent place among the Panch Kedars.

Historical Significance

The history of Kedarnath Temple is steeped in mythology and devotion. According to legend, it was initially built by the Pandavas, the heroic figures from the Mahabharata, to seek Lord Shiva's blessings for redemption after the Kurukshetra war. Lord Shiva, disguised as a bull, evaded the Pandavas, but his hump was found at the site where the temple now stands.

The current structure is attributed to Adi Shankaracharya, who is believed to have reconstructed it in the 8th century CE. Over the centuries, the temple has withstood natural calamities like earthquakes and avalanches, with its most recent restoration believed to have been in the 19th century.

Cultural Activities

Daily rituals at the Kedarnath Temple are performed by the Rawal, the head priest. Visitors can participate in the Maha Abhishek at 4:00 AM or attend the evening Shyan Aarti at 7:00 PM. Devotees can book various types of pujas, including special ceremonies for specific occasions.

The temple is not only a spiritual hub but also a cultural beacon. Festivals like Mahashivratri attract thousands of devotees. The architecture of the temple, with its intricately interlocked stone slabs, is a testament to ancient North Indian craftsmanship.
